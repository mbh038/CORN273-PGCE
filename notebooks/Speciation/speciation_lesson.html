Speciation

Speciation: Mechanisms of Biodiversity Generation

Level 5 Applied Zoology & Conservation - 2 Hour Lesson Plan

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Define speciation and explain its role in generating biodiversity/
  2. Distinguish between allopatric, sympatric, parapatric, and peripatric speciation/
  3. Identify geographic and reproductive barriers that facilitate speciation/
  4. Apply speciation concepts to real-world conservation scenarios
  5. Evaluate evidence for different/ speciation mechanisms in case studies /

PART 1: Introduction & Core Concepts (30 minutes)

Opening Hook (5 minutes)

Discussion Question: “We’ve established that biodiversity supports ecosystem resilience. But where does this diversity come from? How do we get ~10 million species from common ancestors?”

What is Speciation? (10 minutes)

Definition: The evolutionary process by which populations evolve to become distinct species.

Key Concept - The Biological Species Concept (BSC) - Species = groups of interbreeding natural populations that are reproductively isolated from other such groups

Limitations to discuss:

  1. Asexual organisms

  2. Ring species eg Ensatina salamanders in California - like Chinese whispers

  3. Chronospecies (fossils)

  4. Microorganisms with horizontal gene transfer

Critical Requirements for Speciation:

  1. Reproductive isolation (reduced gene flow)

  2. Genetic divergence (populations accumulate different mutations)

  3. Time (typically thousands to millions of years)

The Speciation Continuum (15 minutes)

Important Framework: Speciation is a process, not an event. Populations exist on a continuum:

Panmixia → Population structure → Subspecies → Incipient species → Good species
(Complete gene flow) ←――――――――――――――――――――――――――――→ (Complete isolation)

Interactive Activity: Place these examples on the continuum

  • Herring gulls (Larus argentatus)

  • ring species - Ensatina salamanders in California

  • Polar bears and grizzly bears (fertile hybrids possible)

  • Lions and tigers (hybrids usually sterile)

  • European and American robins (different genera, no interbreeding)

Discussion point:

Why is this continuum important for conservation? (Hint: ESUs - Evolutionarily Significant Units)

PART 2: Geographic Modes of Speciation (40 minutes)

1. Allopatric Speciation (15 minutes)

Definition: Speciation occurring when populations are geographically separated

Classic Model:

  1. Continuous population → geographic barrier arises

  2. Gene flow ceases between populations

  3. Populations diverge through drift, different selection pressures, different mutations

  4. Reproductive isolation evolves (often as byproduct)

  5. Even if barrier removed, populations no longer interbreed

Examples to Discuss:

Example A - Darwin’s Finches (Galápagos)

  • 18 species evolved from single colonizing species

  • Island isolation + different ecological niches

  • Divergence in beak morphology related to food sources

  • Some islands have multiple species (secondary contact)

Example B - Grand Canyon Squirrels

  • Kaibab squirrel (north rim) vs. Abert’s squirrel (south rim)

  • Separated ~5 million years ago

  • Distinct color patterns, slight size differences

  • Status: subspecies or full species? (Debated - demonstrates continuum!)

Conservation Relevance:

  • Habitat fragmentation can initiate speciation processes

  • But modern fragmentation too rapid for adaptation

  • Creates isolated populations vulnerable to extinction

Student Exercise 1 (5 min): The Isthmus of Panama formed ~3 million years ago, separating Caribbean and Pacific populations of marine organisms. Predict what factors would influence whether sister species on either side show complete reproductive isolation today.

2. Peripatric Speciation (8 minutes)

Definition: Special case of allopatric speciation where a small population at the edge of the range becomes isolated

Key Mechanism - Founder Effect:

  • Small population = reduced genetic diversity

  • Genetic drift more powerful in small populations

  • Rapid divergence possible

Example - Hawaiian Drosophila:

  • ~1,000 species evolved from 1-2 founding species

  • Island hopping creates serial founder events

  • Some species restricted to single volcanic cone

  • Fastest known rates of speciation in animals

Why Different from Standard Allopatric?

  • Asymmetric process (small vs. large population)

  • Greater role for drift

  • Potentially faster

3. Parapatric Speciation (8 minutes)

Definition: Speciation occurring along an environmental gradient where populations are adjacent but gene flow is reduced (not eliminated)

Key Feature: No complete geographic barrier, but selection against migrants or hybrids

Example - Grass on Mine Tailings (Anthoxanthum odoratum):

  • Grasses evolved heavy metal tolerance on contaminated soil

  • Distinct populations within metres of each other

  • Some gene flow occurs but selection is strong

  • Flowering time divergence reduces gene flow further

Example - Alpine-Lowland Divergence:

  • Many examples in mountain systems

  • Altitudinal gradients create different selection pressures

  • Limited gene flow due to adaptation to local conditions

Discussion: What level of gene flow prevents parapatric speciation? (Theoretical threshold: ~1 migrant per generation can homogenize populations)

4. Sympatric Speciation (9 minutes)

Definition: Speciation within a single population in the same geographic area

Controversial Because: Gene flow should homogenize the population

Mechanisms That Make It Possible:

A. Polyploidy (especially in plants)

  • Instant reproductive isolation

  • Autopolyploidy: chromosome doubling within species

  • Allopolyploidy: hybridization + chromosome doubling

  • ~50-70% of flowering plants have polyploidy in their history

B. Disruptive Selection + Assortative Mating

  • Strong selection for extreme phenotypes

  • Individuals preferentially mate with similar phenotypes

  • Reduces gene flow between emerging groups

Example - Cichlid Fishes (African Lakes):

  • Hundreds of species in single lakes (e.g., Lake Victoria: ~500 species)

  • Evidence for in-situ speciation

  • Divergence in color, morphology, feeding ecology

  • Mate choice based on coloration (assortative mating)

  • Debate: truly sympatric or microallopatric?

Example - Apple Maggot Fly (Rhagoletis pomonella):

  • Originally fed on hawthorn

  • ~150 years ago, some shifted to introduced apples

  • Host plant choice linked to mating site

  • Behavioral isolation emerging

  • “Speciation in action”

BREAK (10 minutes)

PART 3: Reproductive Barriers (25 minutes)

Framework: Prezygotic vs. Postzygotic Barriers (5 minutes)

Prezygotic (prevent mating or fertilization):

  1. Habitat/ecological isolation

  2. Temporal isolation

  3. Behavioral isolation

  4. Mechanical isolation

  5. Gametic isolation

Postzygotic (reduce hybrid fitness):

  1. Hybrid inviability

  2. Hybrid sterility

  3. Hybrid breakdown

Question for Class: Which type is “better” from an evolutionary perspective? Why? (Answer: Prezygotic - less wasted reproductive effort)

Interactive Exercise: Barrier Identification (20 minutes)

Activity: Students work in pairs to identify barriers in case studies

Case Study 1 - European Fire-bellied Toad and Yellow-bellied Toad:

  • Narrow hybrid zone (~10 km wide) has persisted for thousands of years

  • Hybrids are viable but slightly less fit

  • What maintains this zone?

  • What barriers are operating?

Case Study 2 - Eastern and Western Meadowlarks (North America):

  • Broadly overlapping ranges

  • Very similar appearance

  • Different songs

  • Rarely hybridize

  • What type of isolation? What might have initiated divergence?

Case Study 3 - Blue Mussels (Mytilus spp.):

  • Two species overlap on Atlantic coasts

  • Spawn at different temperatures

  • Hybrids occur but are selected against in intermediate habitats

  • What barriers? What mode of speciation?

Case Study 4 - Palms on Lord Howe Island:

  • Howea belmoreana (acid volcanic soil) and H. forsteriana (calcareous coralderived soil)

  • Separated by ~100m in places

  • Flower at different times (5 weeks apart)

  • Likely sympatric speciation

  • What barriers? Why didn’t gene flow prevent divergence?

Discussion Points:

  • Multiple barriers usually accumulate

  • Reinforcement: selection strengthens prezygotic barriers in sympatry

  • Cascade effect: one barrier can facilitate others

PART 4: Time Scales and Rates (15 minutes)

How Long Does Speciation Take? (8 minutes)

Short Answer: It varies enormously!

Fast End:

  • Some cichlids: <100,000 years

  • Polyploid plants: instantaneous reproductive isolation (but still need ecological divergence)

  • Hawaiian Drosophila: average ~500,000 years per species

Slow End:

  • Many marine invertebrates: 2-10 million years

  • Some “living fossils” show little change over tens of millions of years

Factors Affecting Rate:

  • Generation time

  • Population size (genetic drift)

  • Strength of selection

  • Geographic opportunity (islands, fragmentation)

  • Reproductive system (polyploidy, sexual selection)

Student Challenge Question (7 minutes)

The Speciation Puzzle:

“Imagine two populations of beetles that become separated by a new river. After 1 million years, you bring them back together in the lab and find they produce healthy, fertile offspring. Are they different species?”

Follow-up questions:

  • What additional information would you want?

  • Does it matter if they would interbreed in nature?

  • How does this relate to the speciation continuum?

  • What are the conservation implications?

Key Teaching Point: Species concepts are human constructs trying to categorize a continuous natural process. Different concepts are useful in different contexts (taxonomy, conservation, ecology).

PART 5: Conservation Applications (15 minutes)

Why Does Understanding Speciation Matter for Conservation? (5 minutes)

Discussion points:

  1. Defining units of conservation: What should we protect? - Species? Subspecies? ESUs? Populations?

  2. Habitat fragmentation: Does it increase or decrease diversity?

    • Short term: reduces diversity (extinctions)
    • Long term: could increase diversity (speciation)
    • But: current fragmentation too rapid for speciation

  3. Hybrid zones: To protect or not?

    • Source of genetic diversity?
    • Or dilution of distinct gene pools?

  4. Island conservation: Special considerations

    • High endemism (unique species)
    • Products of isolation
    • Vulnerable to invasive species

Case Study Discussion: The Red Wolf Problem (10 minutes)

Background:

  • Red wolf (Canis rufus) declared extinct in wild (1980), captive breeding program started

  • Recently reintroduced to North Carolina in US

  • Genetic studies suggest red wolves might be hybrids between gray wolves and coyotes

  • Debate: Is the red wolf a distinct species or an admixed population?

Questions for Student Groups:

  1. If red wolves are of hybrid origin, should we still conserve them?

  2. How does the speciation continuum concept apply here?

  3. What criteria should we use for conservation decisions?

  4. Does evolutionary history matter, or just current distinctiveness?

No Right Answer - this is an active debate in conservation biology!

Teaching Point: Conservation often operates in the “gray areas” of speciation. Need clear frameworks for decision-making that consider:

  • Adaptive uniqueness

  • Ecological role

  • Evolutionary potential

  • Legal/social factors

PART 6: Synthesis & Looking Forward (10 minutes)

Connecting to Future Content (5 minutes)

Preview: “We’ll next explore the genetic mechanisms underlying speciation”:

  • What genes cause reproductive isolation?

  • How many genetic changes are needed?

  • Role of chromosome rearrangements

  • Genomic islands of divergence

  • Speciation genes

Bridge to Community Ecology:

  • Speciation creates the diversity we measure

  • Rates of speciation vs. extinction determine community diversity

  • Ecological opportunity can drive adaptive radiation

  • Priority effects in community assembly

Concept Check Questions (5 minutes)

Quick Quiz (can be done as clickers, show-of-hands, or written):

  1. Which speciation mode requires complete geographic separation?
  2. True/False: Sympatric speciation is impossible in animals.
  3. Name two prezygotic barriers.
  4. Why are island systems hotspots of speciation?
  5. Can speciation be reversed?

Answers:

  1. Allopatric

  2. False (cichlids, apple maggot fly, though debated)

  3. Any two: habitat, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, gametic

  4. Geographic isolation + ecological opportunity + often small populations

  5. Yes! If gene flow resumes before complete isolation (e.g., “despeciation” in Darwin’s finches during drought)

Take-Home Problem Set

Due next session - to be discussed in small groups:

Problem 1: You discover a new island with a radiation of 15 bird species, all descended from a single colonist 2 million years ago. Describe what patterns you might expect in: (a) geographic distributions, (b) morphological traits, (c) genetic relationships.

Problem 2: Propose an experiment to test whether two similar butterfly populations represent incipient species. What would you measure? What results would support the hypothesis that they are diverging?

Problem 3: Climate change is causing many species to shift their ranges poleward. How might this affect: (a) existing hybrid zones, (b) opportunities for speciation, (c) conservation of endemic species?

Problem 4: The British Isles were recolonized after the last ice age (~10,000 years ago). Why don’t we see extensive speciation events in British fauna compared to, say, African rift lake fishes?

Notes for Instructor

Anticipated Student Difficulties:

  • Confusing isolation with speciation: Emphasize that isolation is necessary but not sufficient

  • Binary thinking about species: Keep returning to the continuum concept

  • Timescales: Help students grasp deep time - use analogies

  • Assuming all speciation is allopatric: Emphasize diversity of mechanisms

Extensions if Time Permits:

  • Ring species in detail (Ensatina, Larus gulls)

  • Reinforcement and character displacement

  • Role of sexual selection in speciation

  • Hybrid speciation

  • Ancient vs. recent polyploidy

Adjustments for Different Levels:

  • If students struggle: focus primarily on allopatric speciation, use more concrete examples

  • If students are advanced: introduce genetics earlier, discuss speciation gene research

Assessment Opportunities:

  • This material lends itself well to exam questions requiring application

  • Case studies for essays

  • Practical sessions examining hybrid zones or ring species data
